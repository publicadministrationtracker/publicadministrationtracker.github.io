Design Worksheet

Use this section as a civic design tool. The goal is not to start with a preferred ideology or party outcome, but to clarify the public objective, diagnose the failure, and identify reforms that can realistically be implemented.

Part I — Core Democratic Design Objectives

A reform proposal should be judged against clear democratic objectives. These objectives can help separate reforms that merely change who wins from reforms that improve the public system itself.

Representation

Fair public representation

Constituents should be represented in ways that reflect public preferences, communities, and legitimate minority viewpoints without creating permanent minority rule.

Accountability

Accountability during the term

Officials should remain answerable throughout their term, not only during campaign season. Public performance should be visible, measurable, and reviewable.

Trust

Transparent public process

Citizens should be able to understand how decisions are made, who influences them, and what evidence is used to justify public action.

Anti-Capture

People ahead of money

Campaign finance, lobbying, and conflict-of-interest rules should reduce the ability of concentrated wealth to dominate public decision-making.

Responsiveness

Stable but responsive institutions

Government should be durable enough to function, but responsive enough to correct course when evidence, public needs, or abuses require action.

Participation

Participation beyond voting

Citizens should have meaningful ways to deliberate, provide feedback, review performance, and help shape public priorities between elections.

Part II — Current Failure Modes

Reform design should begin with diagnosis. Different failures require different remedies; no single reform fixes every weakness in a democratic system.

Representation failures

Gerrymandering, winner-take-all distortions, uncompetitive districts, geographic sorting, and primary systems that reward narrow political incentives.

Accountability failures

Elections are infrequent and media-driven, while day-to-day governance performance is often difficult for ordinary citizens to evaluate.

Information failures

Citizens face misinformation, low-quality political communication, inaccessible public data, and too few plain-language explanations of public decisions.

Financial influence failures

Constant fundraising, independent expenditure groups, lobbying opacity, and revolving-door incentives can separate public policy from public interest.

Administrative failures

Public agencies may lack clear outcome metrics, user-friendly transparency systems, and independent review mechanisms that allow timely correction.

Participation failures

Many people are only asked to participate at election time, even though public problems require continuous learning, feedback, and adjustment.

Part IV — 21st Century Accountability & Transparency Systems

A modern reform framework should make elections less like the only public accountability event and more like one piece of a continuous accountability system.

Public performance dashboards

Track legislative activity, agency outcomes, public spending, constituent service response times, and progress on major commitments.

Citizen review panels

Use structured, representative panels to review major issues, agency performance, or reform proposals and publish public recommendations.

Plain-language bill tracking

Translate proposed laws, amendments, fiscal notes, votes, and implementation status into readable public summaries.

Ethics and conflict databases

Make financial disclosures, recusals, gifts, lobbying contacts, and conflict-of-interest records searchable and understandable.

Administrative scorecards

Evaluate public agencies by outcomes, service quality, timeliness, equity, budget stewardship, and responsiveness to corrective findings.

Public correction loops

Create formal processes for citizens, watchdogs, auditors, and journalists to flag errors, delays, abuses, or unmet public commitments.

The core shift

Election reform should not only change how officials are selected. It should also change how public power is monitored, explained, corrected, and held accountable after officials take office.

Part V — Reform Roadmap

A reform roadmap should clarify who has authority, what legal pathway is available, what resistance is likely, and how the public can evaluate whether the reform actually worked.

Define the failure

Name the specific democratic failure: unfair districts, low accountability, money influence, inaccessible data, weak ethics enforcement, or poor public participation.

Identify the authority

Determine whether the reform belongs at the city, county, state, federal, agency, court, or ballot-initiative level.

Match the reform tool

Select a reform that directly addresses the failure. Avoid reforms that sound appealing but do not solve the diagnosed problem.

Check legal feasibility

Separate reforms that can be done by ordinance, statute, administrative action, or ballot measure from reforms that require deeper constitutional change.

Design transparency metrics

Decide how the public will know whether the reform improved fairness, representation, accountability, participation, or trust.

Build public legitimacy

Use plain-language education, public hearings, citizen panels, pilot programs, and nonpartisan evaluation to reduce suspicion and improve adoption.
